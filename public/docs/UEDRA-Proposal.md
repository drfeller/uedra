## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Estate planning documents -- wills, trusts, powers of attorney, advance directives, and related instruments -- are among the most consequential legal documents any individual will execute. Yet they remain the only major category of legal instruments in the United States for which no systematic recording, registration, or verification framework exists. Real property transfers have been subject to recording acts since the colonial era. Security interests are perfected through UCC filings. Vehicle titles are tracked by state motor vehicle agencies. Birth and death certificates are maintained in vital records systems. Estate planning documents, by contrast, exist in a regulatory void: once executed, they are entrusted to desk drawers, safe deposit boxes, and the fallible memory of family members and counsel.

The Uniform Law Commission took an important step in 2019 with the Uniform Electronic Estate Planning Documents Act (UEEPDA), which established that estate planning documents executed, signed, and stored electronically satisfy state formality requirements. Sixteen states have now adopted UEEPDA. But electronic equivalence, standing alone, does not solve the fundamental accessibility, verification, and integrity problems that plague estate planning documents. An electronically executed will that cannot be located at the testator's death is no more useful than a paper will lost in a house fire.

This proposal asks the Committee on Scope and Program to appoint a Study Committee to examine whether a Uniform Estate Planning Document Registry Act -- a natural companion to UEEPDA -- should be drafted to establish consistent standards for (1) the voluntary registration of estate planning documents, (2) identity and document integrity verification at the point of registration, (3) authorized access and verification query procedures, (4) interstate recognition of registered documents, and (5) the certification of qualified registry providers, whether public or private. The registry model is proven: forty-six states now maintain registries for Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST), demonstrating both the feasibility and the life-saving utility of making critical planning documents accessible when they are needed. It is time to extend this model to the full spectrum of estate planning instruments.

## II. STATEMENT OF THE PROBLEM

### A. The Registration Gap in American Law

The American legal system has, over centuries, developed recording and registration systems for virtually every category of important legal instrument -- with one glaring exception. Estate planning documents occupy a unique and indefensible position: they are the only major class of legally operative instruments for which the law provides no mechanism for recording, registration, indexing, or verification.

Consider the infrastructure that exists for comparable instruments:

**Real Property.** Recording acts have existed in the United States since the Massachusetts Bay Colony established its system in 1640. Today, every county in every state maintains a recording office where deeds, mortgages, liens, and easements are indexed, searchable, and available for public inspection. The entire system of real property title assurance -- title insurance, title searches, marketable title acts -- depends on these recording systems.

**Security Interests.** Article 9 of the Uniform Commercial Code, adopted in all fifty states, establishes a comprehensive filing system for security interests in personal property. UCC financing statements are filed with the Secretary of State, creating a searchable public record that puts third parties on notice and establishes priority among competing claims.

**Vehicle Titles.** Every state maintains a centralized title registry for motor vehicles through its department of motor vehicles. Title certificates, liens, and transfers are recorded, creating a reliable chain of ownership.

**Vital Records.** Birth certificates, death certificates, and marriage licenses are recorded in state vital records systems, with standardized data formats and interstate reporting mechanisms coordinated through the National Center for Health Statistics.

**Corporate and Business Filings.** Articles of incorporation, partnership agreements, and LLC formations are filed with the Secretary of State, creating public records of entity existence, registered agents, and organizational structure.

Against this backdrop, the absence of any registration system for estate planning documents is remarkable. A will disposing of a multimillion-dollar estate receives less systematic treatment than a $500 UCC financing statement for inventory. A durable power of attorney authorizing control over an elderly person's life savings has no recording mechanism, while a lien on a used automobile is meticulously tracked. An advance directive expressing an individual's most fundamental healthcare wishes has no assured means of reaching the physicians who need it, while a corporate annual report is reliably on file with the Secretary of State.

### B. The "Lost Will" Crisis

The consequences of this gap are not theoretical. The American Bar Association has estimated that a significant percentage of wills are never located after the testator's death. When a valid will cannot be found, the decedent's estate passes by intestacy -- often to unintended beneficiaries and in unintended proportions. The result is a quiet epidemic of frustrated intent, family conflict, and unnecessary litigation.

The problem is exacerbated by modern demographics. Americans are more mobile than at any point in history. Individuals frequently execute estate planning documents in one state, move to another, own property in a third, and die in a fourth. A will executed in New York and stored in an attorney's office may be effectively invisible to the decedent's family in Arizona. An attorney who prepared a will may have retired, relocated, or died, taking knowledge of the document's existence and location with them.

Digital storage has not solved this problem and in some respects has worsened it. Electronic documents stored on password-protected devices, in cloud accounts with unknown credentials, or in email attachments may be even less discoverable than paper documents in a filing cabinet.

### C. Power of Attorney Fraud and Abuse

Powers of attorney present a distinct but equally serious set of problems. Financial exploitation of the elderly is among the fastest-growing categories of fraud in the United States, and the misuse of powers of attorney is a primary vector. The Government Accountability Office, state attorneys general, and elder law practitioners have repeatedly identified the following pattern: an individual obtains a power of attorney from a vulnerable person, often through undue influence, and then uses it to liquidate accounts, transfer property, or redirect income.

Financial institutions face an impossible dilemma. They are liable if they honor a fraudulent or revoked power of attorney, and they are liable if they refuse to honor a valid one. Without a registry, there is no reliable mechanism for an institution to verify that a presented power of attorney is (1) authentic, (2) currently in force and not revoked, and (3) the most recent version executed by the principal. The Uniform Power of Attorney Act (2006) addressed many substantive issues but did not establish a registration or verification system.

### D. Medical Directive Accessibility

The inaccessibility of advance directives in medical emergencies represents a failure with immediate life-and-death consequences. Studies have consistently found that advance directives are unavailable at the time of a medical crisis in a substantial share of cases, even when the patient has executed one. Hospitals, emergency departments, and emergency medical services frequently cannot locate a patient's advance directive, living will, or healthcare power of attorney when time-critical decisions must be made.

The success of POLST registries -- now operating in forty-six states -- demonstrates both the severity of the problem and the efficacy of the registry solution. Oregon's POLST Registry, one of the earliest, has been credited with significantly improving concordance between patient wishes and end-of-life care. But POLST registries cover only a narrow subset of advance planning documents, typically limited to physician-signed orders for patients with serious illness. The broader universe of advance directives, living wills, and healthcare powers of attorney remains outside any registration system.

### E. Interstate Complications

The interstate dimension of the problem is particularly acute and particularly suited to uniform law treatment. Consider a common scenario: an individual executes a will and power of attorney in State A, retires to State B, owns a vacation property in State C, and is hospitalized while traveling in State D. The documents may be physically or electronically stored in State A. The healthcare providers in State D have no mechanism to locate them. The financial institution in State C has no mechanism to verify the power of attorney. The probate court in State B has no mechanism to confirm whether the will presented is the most recent version.

No individual state can solve this problem through legislation alone. A registry established by State A is useful only if State D recognizes it and has a mechanism to query it. Uniform legislation is the only realistic path to a functional interstate system.

### F. Economic Costs

The economic costs of the current regime are substantial, though difficult to quantify precisely. They include: the direct costs of contested probate proceedings where the existence, authenticity, or currency of a will is at issue; the costs of intestate administration when a valid will exists but cannot be found; the costs of financial exploitation enabled by unverifiable powers of attorney; the costs of unwanted medical treatment delivered because advance directives were unavailable; duplicative court proceedings in multiple jurisdictions; and the administrative costs borne by financial institutions, healthcare providers, and courts in attempting to verify documents for which no verification infrastructure exists.

## III. EXISTING LEGAL LANDSCAPE

### A. The Uniform Electronic Estate Planning Documents Act (UEEPDA)

The Uniform Law Commission approved UEEPDA in 2019, establishing that estate planning documents -- including wills, trusts, and powers of attorney -- may be executed, attested, witnessed, notarized, and stored using electronic means, provided that the applicable state's substantive execution requirements are otherwise satisfied. As of early 2026, sixteen states have enacted UEEPDA or substantially similar legislation: Colorado, Florida, Idaho, Indiana, Iowa, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, Montana, North Dakota, South Dakota, Utah, Virginia, Washington, and the District of Columbia, with additional states having introduced legislation.

UEEPDA was a necessary and important step. But it addressed only the *medium* of estate planning documents -- establishing that electronic formats are legally equivalent to paper -- without addressing the *infrastructure* for managing those documents once executed. UEEPDA says nothing about how to register, locate, verify, or share estate planning documents. It created electronic equivalence but left the accessibility and verification gap entirely unaddressed.

### B. State Will Depositories

A small number of states have established mechanisms for depositing wills with a state or local government office, but these systems are fragmented, limited, and largely analog:

- **Indiana** permits wills to be deposited with the clerk of the circuit court during the testator's lifetime.
- **Wisconsin** allows deposit with the register in probate in the county of the testator's domicile.
- **Texas** permits deposit with the county clerk, though the system is lightly used.
- **Delaware** authorizes the Register of Wills to accept wills for safekeeping.
- **Maryland** allows deposit with the Register of Wills in any county.
- **Washington** permits deposit with the county clerk.

These depositories share common limitations: they typically accept only wills (not the full range of estate planning documents); they store metadata or physical documents rather than providing searchable, verifiable digital registries; they operate at the county level with no statewide consolidation; they lack any interstate interoperability; and they predate the digital era in design and functionality.

### C. POLST and Advance Directive Registries

The most successful model for registry-based access to planning documents is the POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) registry system. Forty-six states and the District of Columbia have established POLST programs, and a growing number maintain electronic registries that allow healthcare providers to access physician orders in real time.

Oregon's POLST Registry, launched in 2009, is widely regarded as the national model. It stores POLST forms electronically and makes them available to healthcare providers through a secure query system, with access controls and audit trails. California established its registry through SB-19, and Maryland mandated a statewide registry through subsequent legislation. The National POLST Collaborative coordinates standards across states.

These registries demonstrate several critical principles: (1) a registry model for sensitive planning documents is technically and legally feasible; (2) healthcare providers will use registries when they exist; (3) appropriate access controls can balance accessibility with privacy; and (4) the registry model saves lives by ensuring that documents are available when needed. However, POLST registries are limited to physician-signed orders for a subset of patients. The far larger universe of advance directives, living wills, and healthcare powers of attorney remains unregistered.

### D. Recent State Legislative Activity

Recent legislative activity confirms growing interest in estate document registration:

- **Illinois** has considered SB 3291 and HB 4722, which would establish a state-level will depository system.
- **North Carolina** enacted Session Law 2025-33, authorizing the electronic storage of wills under specified conditions.
- **New York** enacted the Electronic Wills Act in December 2025, permitting electronic execution and storage of wills with supervised procedures.

These individual state efforts, while welcome, underscore the need for uniform treatment. Each state is developing its own approach, with its own standards, its own technology requirements, and its own access protocols. Without uniform legislation, the result will be a patchwork of incompatible systems that fail to address the interstate dimension of the problem.

### E. Private-Sector Initiatives

The U.S. Will Registry operates as a nonprofit service that records metadata about the existence and location of wills and estate documents. It does not store the documents themselves; it records the fact that a document exists and the name and contact information of the attorney who prepared it. While valuable as a locator service, the U.S. Will Registry has no legal authority, creates no legal presumptions, and provides no document integrity verification.

The absence of a legal framework for document registration means that private-sector initiatives, however well-intentioned, cannot provide the legal certainty that a statutory registry could offer. A registered document should carry legal weight -- a presumption of authenticity, a presumption that it is the most recent version, and a reliable chain of custody. Only legislation can create these effects.

## IV. WHY UNIFORM LAW IS NEEDED

### A. Interstate Recognition

The most compelling argument for uniform legislation, rather than individual state action, is the inherently interstate nature of the problem. Estate planning documents routinely cross state lines: individuals execute documents in one state and die in another; powers of attorney are presented to financial institutions in states other than the state of execution; advance directives must be accessible wherever the patient receives care. A registry system that operates only within a single state's borders addresses only a fraction of the problem. Uniform legislation would establish reciprocal recognition: a document registered in any adopting state's compliant registry would be recognized in all adopting states.

### B. Consistent Verification Standards

Without uniform standards, each state developing a registry will establish its own identity verification requirements, its own document integrity standards, and its own access protocols. The result will be a patchwork in which a document registered in one state may not meet the verification standards of another. Uniform legislation would establish minimum standards for identity verification, document integrity (including cryptographic hash verification), and metadata recording, ensuring that a registration in any compliant system meets a consistent baseline of reliability.

### C. Interoperability

A uniform act would mandate technical interoperability standards, ensuring that state registries -- whether operated by public agencies or certified private providers -- can communicate with one another. Interoperability is essential for interstate queries: when a hospital in Florida needs to determine whether a patient from Michigan has a registered advance directive, the systems must be able to exchange information reliably and securely. The POLST registry system has demonstrated both the importance and the feasibility of interstate interoperability standards.

### D. Consumer Protection

The absence of a registration framework leaves consumers vulnerable to document fraud, unauthorized alterations, and exploitation. A uniform registry act would provide critical consumer protections: version control to prevent the presentation of superseded documents; audit trails to detect unauthorized access; notification mechanisms to alert registrants when their documents are queried; and penalties for fraudulent registration or unauthorized access. These protections are especially important for elderly and vulnerable populations targeted by financial exploitation.

### E. Reducing the Burden on Courts and Institutions

Probate courts, financial institutions, healthcare providers, and government agencies currently bear significant costs in attempting to verify estate planning documents without any verification infrastructure. Courts adjudicate disputes over the authenticity and currency of wills. Banks develop ad hoc procedures for evaluating powers of attorney. Hospitals make life-and-death decisions without access to advance directives. A uniform registry would reduce these costs by providing a reliable, standardized mechanism for document verification.

### F. Natural Companion to UEEPDA

UEEPDA established that estate planning documents may exist in electronic form. A Uniform Estate Planning Document Registry Act would establish where those electronic documents are kept, how they are verified, and how authorized parties access them. The two acts are natural companions: UEEPDA created the digital document; UEDRA would create the digital infrastructure. Together, they would constitute a comprehensive framework for estate planning documents in the twenty-first century.

## V. PROPOSED SCOPE OF THE ACT

The following sections outline the key provisions that a drafting committee should consider. These are presented as a framework for study, not as final legislative text.

### A. Definitions

The act should establish clear definitions for its core concepts:

- **"Registry"** -- A system, whether operated by a government agency or a certified private provider, that accepts and maintains the registration of estate planning documents and responds to authorized verification queries.
- **"Registered Document"** -- An estate planning document that has been submitted to and accepted by a registry in accordance with the act's registration requirements.
- **"Registrant"** -- The individual who executed the estate planning document and who submits it, or authorizes its submission, for registration.
- **"Authorized Registry Provider"** -- A government agency designated by the state, or a private entity certified by the state, to operate a registry in compliance with the act.
- **"Verification Query"** -- A request submitted by an authorized party to a registry to determine whether a document has been registered, to verify the authenticity or currency of a presented document, or to obtain access to a registered document.
- **"Supersession"** -- The registration of a subsequent version of a document that replaces a previously registered version of the same type.

### B. Document Types Eligible for Registration

The act should encompass the full range of estate planning instruments, including but not limited to:

1. **Last wills and testaments**, including codicils
2. **Revocable and irrevocable trusts**, including amendments and restatements
3. **Durable powers of attorney** for financial matters
4. **Healthcare powers of attorney** and healthcare proxies
5. **Advance directives** and living wills
6. **Do-not-resuscitate (DNR) orders** and POLST forms (to the extent not already covered by existing registries)
7. **Guardian and conservator nominations**
8. **Beneficiary designations** for non-probate transfers
9. **Revocation instruments** for any of the foregoing

The act should be technology-neutral, accepting documents in both electronic and paper-original form (with paper documents digitized upon registration). The act should explicitly provide that registration is *voluntary* and that the failure to register a document does not impair its validity or enforceability.

### C. Registry Administration

The act should provide states with flexibility in how they administer their registries, recognizing that states have different institutional capacities, budgetary constraints, and policy preferences. Specifically, the act should authorize two models:

1. **Public Registry Model.** A state may designate an existing government agency -- such as the Secretary of State, the state court administrator, or county clerks -- to operate the registry directly.

2. **Certified Private Provider Model.** A state may certify one or more private entities to operate registries on behalf of the state, provided that the private provider meets the act's technical, security, and operational standards. Under this model, the state retains regulatory oversight and certification authority while leveraging private-sector technology and infrastructure.

3. **Hybrid Model.** A state may employ a combination of public operation and private infrastructure, such as a state-branded registry operated on technology infrastructure provided by a certified private provider.

This flexibility is essential. Many states lack the technical capacity or budgetary resources to build and operate a sophisticated digital registry from scratch. The certified private provider model allows states to adopt the act without significant capital expenditure by leveraging existing private-sector infrastructure that meets state-mandated standards. At the same time, states that prefer direct government operation retain that option.

### D. Registration Requirements

The act should establish minimum requirements for registration, ensuring that every registered document meets a baseline of verified authenticity. These requirements should include:

#### 1. Identity Verification

The registrant's identity must be verified at the time of registration through means that provide reasonable assurance of identity. The act should establish minimum standards while allowing flexibility in implementation:

- Government-issued photo identification verified through document authentication technology
- Knowledge-based authentication or credential-based verification
- Notarization or attestation by a licensed attorney
- Multi-factor authentication for electronic submissions

#### 2. Document Integrity Verification

At the time of registration, a cryptographic hash of the document must be generated and recorded. This hash serves as a digital fingerprint that can later be used to verify that a document presented as a registered document has not been altered since registration. The act should specify minimum hashing standards (e.g., SHA-256 or equivalent).

#### 3. Metadata Recording

The registry must record, at minimum, the following metadata for each registered document:

- Document type (will, trust, power of attorney, etc.)
- Date of execution
- Registrant name and identifying information
- Names of witnesses and notary (if applicable)
- Notarization status
- Name of the attorney who prepared the document (if applicable)
- Date of registration
- Unique registration identifier
- Cryptographic hash of the document
- Supersession information (if the document replaces a previously registered version)

#### 4. Version Control and Supersession

The act must address the critical issue of document supersession. When a registrant registers a new will, power of attorney, or other document, the registry must maintain the chain of versions:

- The most recent registered version of a document type should be clearly identified
- Previously registered versions should be retained but flagged as superseded
- The registry should record the date, method, and identity verification associated with each supersession
- Revocation of a registered document should be registrable through a defined procedure

#### 5. Enhanced Verification Tier

The act may provide for an optional enhanced verification tier in which an attorney attests to the document's execution, the registrant's capacity, and compliance with applicable state formalities. Documents registered with attorney attestation could receive a stronger evidentiary presumption.

### E. Legal Effect of Registration

The legal effect of registration is among the most important and sensitive issues the drafting committee must address. The act should carefully calibrate the legal consequences of registration to provide meaningful benefits without disrupting existing substantive law. The following framework is proposed for study:

#### 1. Rebuttable Presumption of Currency

A registered document should be entitled to a rebuttable presumption that it is the most recent version of that document type executed by the registrant. This presumption would shift the burden of production (but not the burden of persuasion) to a party asserting that a later, unregistered version exists.

#### 2. Rebuttable Presumption of Authenticity

A registered document should be entitled to a rebuttable presumption of authenticity -- that is, a presumption that the document is what it purports to be, that it was executed by the person identified as the registrant, and that it has not been altered since registration (as verified by the cryptographic hash).

#### 3. No Effect on Underlying Validity

Registration should *not* affect the substantive validity requirements of the underlying document. A will must still be executed in compliance with the applicable state's Wills Act. A power of attorney must still comply with applicable statutory requirements. Registration is an *additional* step that provides evidentiary and accessibility benefits; it does not substitute for compliance with execution formalities. The act should include an explicit provision that registration does not validate an otherwise defective instrument.

#### 4. Revocation

The act must provide clear procedures for the revocation of a registration, including revocation by the registrant, revocation by operation of law (e.g., divorce), and revocation by court order. Revocation of a registration should not, by itself, affect the legal validity of the underlying document.

### F. Access and Verification

The act must carefully balance two competing interests: ensuring that registered documents are accessible to those who need them, and protecting the privacy of registrants and the confidentiality of their estate plans.

#### 1. Authorized Parties

The act should define categories of persons and entities authorized to submit verification queries:

- **The registrant** (full access to own documents at all times)
- **Named agents, trustees, and fiduciaries** identified in the registered document
- **The registrant's attorney of record**
- **Courts** with jurisdiction over the registrant's estate, guardianship, or conservatorship proceedings
- **Financial institutions** presented with a power of attorney purportedly executed by the registrant
- **Healthcare providers** involved in the registrant's care (for advance directives and healthcare powers of attorney)
- **Government agencies** with a statutory basis for access (e.g., adult protective services)

#### 2. Tiered Disclosure

Not all authorized parties need access to the full text of a document. The act should establish tiered disclosure levels:

- **Existence verification** -- Confirmation that a document of a specified type is registered, without disclosing its contents (available to the broadest category of authorized parties)
- **Currency verification** -- Confirmation that a presented document matches the most recently registered version (available to financial institutions, healthcare providers, and courts)
- **Full document access** -- Disclosure of the complete registered document (available to the registrant, named fiduciaries, courts, and other parties with demonstrated need)

#### 3. Emergency Access

The act should include special provisions for emergency access to healthcare-related documents (advance directives, healthcare powers of attorney, POLST forms) by healthcare providers. Emergency access should require minimal procedural burden, recognizing that time-critical medical decisions cannot await normal verification procedures. The POLST registry model provides a well-tested framework for emergency access protocols.

#### 4. Institutional Verification

The act should mandate that authorized registry providers offer a standardized application programming interface (API) for institutional verification queries. This API would allow financial institutions, healthcare systems, courts, and other institutional users to submit verification queries programmatically, enabling integration with existing institutional workflows. API standards should be uniform across all authorized registry providers to ensure interoperability.

### G. Technical Standards

The act should establish minimum technical standards for authorized registry providers, whether public or private. These standards should address:

#### 1. Security

- Data encryption in transit and at rest (minimum AES-256 or equivalent)
- Multi-factor authentication for administrative and registrant access
- Intrusion detection and incident response protocols
- Regular independent security audits

#### 2. Data Integrity

- Cryptographic hash verification for all stored documents
- Immutable audit trails recording all registrations, queries, modifications, and access events
- Blockchain or distributed ledger technology may be employed as an additional integrity mechanism, though the act should remain technology-neutral and not mandate any specific implementation
- Redundant storage with geographic distribution

#### 3. Availability

- Minimum uptime standards for registry availability (e.g., 99.9% availability)
- Disaster recovery and business continuity requirements
- Data backup and restoration procedures

#### 4. Interoperability

- Standardized data formats for document metadata
- Standardized query and response protocols for interstate verification
- API standards for institutional integration
- Compliance with applicable federal standards (HIPAA for healthcare-related documents, NIST cybersecurity frameworks)

### H. Certification of Private Registry Providers

The act should establish a framework for state certification of private registry providers, recognizing that the private sector can provide technology infrastructure, security expertise, and operational capacity that many state agencies cannot match. The certification framework should include:

#### 1. Application and Qualification Requirements

- Demonstrated technical capacity to meet the act's security and operational standards
- SOC 2 Type II certification or equivalent independent security audit
- Financial stability requirements (insurance, bonding, or capital adequacy)
- Organizational governance requirements
- Data privacy and protection commitments
- Demonstrated experience operating information systems of comparable sensitivity and scale

#### 2. Ongoing Compliance

- Annual independent security audits
- Regular compliance reporting to the certifying state agency
- Mandatory breach notification to the state and affected registrants
- Right of state inspection and audit
- Performance metrics and service level obligations

#### 3. Operational Model

The act should explicitly accommodate a model in which a certified private provider operates registry infrastructure that is branded and governed by the state. Under this model:

- The state retains regulatory authority and oversight
- The state determines fee schedules and access policies
- The private provider supplies the technology platform, security infrastructure, and operational support
- Registrants interact with a state-branded system, not a private-sector product
- Data remains under state jurisdiction and subject to state privacy law

This model allows states to adopt a sophisticated digital registry without the capital expenditure, hiring, and technology risk of building a system from scratch, while maintaining full public accountability and oversight.

#### 4. Decertification

The act must provide clear grounds and procedures for decertification of a private provider that fails to meet standards, including provisions for data migration to ensure continuity of the registry and protection of registrant data.

### I. Interstate Recognition

The act should include a full faith and credit provision establishing that:

1. A document registered in any adopting state's compliant registry is entitled to the same legal presumptions (authenticity, currency) in all other adopting states.
2. A verification query submitted from one adopting state to another adopting state's registry must be processed under the same standards applicable to in-state queries.
3. When a registrant moves from one adopting state to another, the registrant may transfer the registration to the new state's registry, and the original registration retains its legal effect during and after the transfer.
4. Courts in adopting states shall give evidentiary weight to registration in another adopting state's compliant registry without requiring re-registration.

### J. Consumer Protections

The act should include robust consumer protections, including:

1. **Fee Limitations.** States should have authority to set maximum fees for registration and verification services, ensuring that cost does not create a barrier to access.
2. **Data Portability.** Registrants must have the right to transfer their registration and associated data to a different authorized registry provider at any time, without penalty.
3. **Access Notification.** Registrants must be notified when their registration is queried, including the identity of the querying party and the type of information disclosed (with exceptions for court-ordered access and law enforcement).
4. **Correction and Amendment.** Registrants must have the right to correct errors in their registration metadata and to update their registration at any time.
5. **Privacy Protections.** The act should establish minimum privacy protections for registrant data, including limitations on the use of registrant data for marketing or commercial purposes unrelated to registry operations.
6. **Penalties.** The act should establish civil and criminal penalties for unauthorized access to registry data, fraudulent registration, and tampering with registered documents.
7. **Right of Action.** Registrants should have a private right of action for violations of the act's privacy and security provisions.

## VI. STAKEHOLDER SUPPORT

A Uniform Estate Planning Document Registry Act would serve the interests of a broad coalition of stakeholders:

**AARP** has long advocated for enhanced protections for older Americans against financial exploitation. A registry that provides verification of powers of attorney and tracks supersession would directly advance AARP's mission of protecting the financial security and autonomy of older adults. AARP supported UEEPDA and has identified document accessibility as a continuing concern.

**The National Academy of Elder Law Attorneys (NAELA)** represents attorneys who practice at the intersection of estate planning, disability planning, and elder law. NAELA members routinely encounter cases in which powers of attorney are misused, advance directives are unavailable, and estate plans are frustrated by the inability to locate documents. NAELA has advocated for stronger verification mechanisms and would be a natural ally of a uniform registry act.

**The American Bar Association Section of Real Property, Trust and Estate Law** has historically engaged actively with ULC estate planning projects, including UEEPDA. The Section's membership includes the practitioners who draft and counsel clients on the very documents that UEDRA would address. A registry act that enhances the reliability and accessibility of estate planning documents serves the interests of both practitioners and their clients.

**The American Hospital Association** and its member institutions have a direct operational interest in the accessibility of advance directives and healthcare powers of attorney. Hospitals currently invest significant resources in attempting to locate patients' advance planning documents, often unsuccessfully. A registry with emergency access provisions would directly reduce this burden and improve patient care.

**The American Bankers Association** has identified power of attorney verification as a persistent challenge for financial institutions. Banks are caught between the risk of honoring a fraudulent or revoked power of attorney and the risk of refusing a valid one. A registry providing real-time verification of power of attorney status would significantly reduce this institutional risk.

**State Bar Associations** would benefit from a system that enhances the value and reliability of the documents their members prepare. Attorneys who counsel clients on estate planning could offer registration as an additional service that increases the likelihood that their clients' wishes are carried out.

**The National POLST Collaborative** has built the infrastructure and institutional knowledge for registering a subset of advance planning documents. The Collaborative's experience and expertise would be invaluable in extending the registry model to the full range of estate planning instruments.

## VII. FISCAL IMPACT AND IMPLEMENTATION

### A. State Implementation Costs

One of the principal advantages of the proposed act's flexible administration model is that states can adopt UEDRA at minimal cost to state treasuries. Under the certified private provider model, the state's role is regulatory -- establishing certification standards, designating a certifying agency, and conducting oversight -- rather than operational. The state need not build, maintain, or operate registry technology infrastructure.

States that prefer direct government operation may leverage existing agency infrastructure. The Secretary of State's office, which in most states already operates UCC filing systems and business entity registries, is a natural fit for an estate document registry. The marginal cost of adding estate document registration to an existing filing infrastructure is substantially less than building a new system.

### B. Fee Structure

Registration and verification fees, paid by registrants and querying parties, can make the registry self-sustaining. POLST registries, UCC filing systems, and real property recording offices all operate on fee-based models that cover or substantially offset their operating costs. The act should authorize states to set fee schedules that cover the costs of administration and oversight while ensuring that fees do not create a barrier to access. States may also consider fee waivers or reduced fees for low-income registrants.

### C. Offset Savings

The costs of operating or overseeing a registry should be weighed against the savings that a registry would generate elsewhere in the system:

- **Probate courts** would see reduced caseloads from contested wills and disputes over document authenticity and currency
- **Financial institutions** would realize savings from reduced fraud losses and reduced compliance costs associated with power of attorney verification
- **Healthcare systems** would benefit from improved advance directive accessibility, potentially reducing unwanted treatments and associated costs
- **Law enforcement and adult protective services** would benefit from audit trails that assist in identifying and prosecuting financial exploitation

### D. Implementation Timeline

The act should provide a reasonable implementation timeline, recognizing that states will need time to designate administering agencies, establish certification procedures, and either build or procure registry infrastructure. A phased implementation -- beginning with will registration and expanding to other document types -- may be appropriate.

## VIII. RELATIONSHIP TO EXISTING ULC ACTS

### A. Uniform Electronic Estate Planning Documents Act (UEEPDA)

UEDRA is designed as a natural companion to UEEPDA. Where UEEPDA establishes that estate planning documents may be created and stored electronically, UEDRA would establish the infrastructure for registering, verifying, and accessing those documents -- whether electronic or paper-original. The two acts are complementary rather than overlapping: UEEPDA governs the *form* of the document; UEDRA governs the *registration* of the document. States that have adopted UEEPDA would find UEDRA particularly straightforward to implement, as the electronic document framework is already in place.

### B. Uniform Probate Code (UPC)

The Uniform Probate Code includes provisions for the deposit of wills with the court (UPC Section 2-515). UEDRA would substantially expand upon and modernize this concept, replacing the analog deposit of a single document type with a comprehensive digital registry for all estate planning instruments. UEDRA should be drafted to coordinate with UPC provisions, including the UPC's rules regarding revocation, the effect of marriage and divorce on testamentary instruments, and the probate of lost or destroyed wills.

### C. Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (UETA)

UETA, adopted in forty-nine states, establishes the general legal equivalence of electronic records and signatures. UEDRA should be consistent with UETA's framework, particularly its provisions regarding electronic signatures, electronic records, and the attribution of electronic communications. UEDRA's technical standards for document integrity verification (cryptographic hashing) are consistent with and build upon UETA's technology-neutral approach to electronic record integrity.

### D. Uniform Power of Attorney Act (UPOAA)

The Uniform Power of Attorney Act (2006) addressed many substantive issues in power of attorney law but did not establish a registration or verification mechanism. UEDRA would fill this gap by providing a registry through which powers of attorney can be registered, verified, and tracked. The combination of UPOAA's substantive protections and UEDRA's verification infrastructure would provide a comprehensive framework for preventing power of attorney fraud.

### E. Uniform Trust Code (UTC)

The Uniform Trust Code governs the creation, modification, and administration of trusts. UEDRA would complement the UTC by providing a mechanism for registering trust instruments (or summaries thereof, given confidentiality concerns unique to trusts). Registration of trust instruments could facilitate verification of trustee authority and reduce disputes over the existence and terms of trusts.

## IX. CONCLUSION AND REQUEST

Estate planning documents are among the most important legal instruments any individual will execute. They direct the disposition of property, authorize the management of financial affairs, and express wishes about medical care at the end of life. Yet the United States has no systematic mechanism for registering, verifying, or locating these documents. The gap is anomalous, costly, and harmful.

The Uniform Law Commission is uniquely positioned to address this problem. The Commission's successful development of UEEPDA demonstrated both the need for and the feasibility of uniform legislation in the estate planning space. UEEPDA solved the *medium* problem -- establishing that estate planning documents may exist in electronic form. The Uniform Estate Planning Document Registry Act would solve the *infrastructure* problem -- establishing where those documents are kept, how they are verified, and how they are accessed when needed.

The registry model is proven. Forty-six states maintain POLST registries. Every state maintains real property recording systems, UCC filing systems, and vital records systems. The technology is mature. The stakeholder support is broad. The need is acute and growing as the population ages, geographic mobility increases, and the volume of estate planning documents in digital form expands.

**The undersigned respectfully request that the Committee on Scope and Program appoint a Study Committee to examine the feasibility and desirability of a Uniform Estate Planning Document Registry Act and to recommend whether the Commission should proceed with a drafting project.**
